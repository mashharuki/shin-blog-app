# Deep-Dive: Project Closing

## Why Closing Matters

Projects that don't close formally leave loose ends: contracts without final invoices, team members
without recognition, lessons that evaporate when people move on, and systems without clear ownership.
A proper close is an act of organizational respect — to the team, the customer, and the next project.

Closing also enforces discipline: it creates accountability for the original objectives and forces
honest assessment of whether the investment delivered its intended value.

---

## Closing Checklist (Comprehensive)

### Deliverables & Acceptance
- [ ] All project deliverables completed and reviewed against acceptance criteria
- [ ] Formal written acceptance received from customer/sponsor for each deliverable
- [ ] Outstanding defects and issues resolved or formally accepted as known limitations
- [ ] Final testing/QA completed and sign-off documented
- [ ] User training completed (if applicable)
- [ ] Go-live or handover completed (if applicable)

### Contracts & Finance
- [ ] All vendor/supplier contracts formally closed
- [ ] Final invoices received, reviewed, and approved
- [ ] Budget reconciled: actual vs. planned with variance explanation
- [ ] Procurement audit completed (if required)
- [ ] Financial close report submitted to finance

### Team & Resources
- [ ] Team performance evaluations completed
- [ ] Individual recognition and celebration organized
- [ ] Team members reassigned or released (resource pool updated)
- [ ] Equipment/tools returned or reallocated
- [ ] Access credentials and permissions revoked

### Knowledge Management
- [ ] Lessons learned sessions facilitated with team and key stakeholders
- [ ] Lessons learned documented in the register and shared with PMO
- [ ] Project documents archived in designated repository
- [ ] Processes and templates updated based on lessons learned (if applicable)
- [ ] Knowledge transfer to operations/support team completed

### Stakeholder Communication
- [ ] Final project status report distributed
- [ ] Project closure announced to all stakeholders
- [ ] Sponsor presented with closure report and formal acceptance
- [ ] Operations team briefed and onboarded (for ongoing systems)

## Lessons Learned Facilitation Guide

### Setting the Right Tone
The most common failure in lessons learned sessions: blame. The goal is to improve the system,
not assign fault. Start every session with this principle:

> "We are here to learn from the past so the next project succeeds more easily.
>  Every problem we discuss was probably caused by a system or process issue, not a person issue."

### Session Format (90 minutes)

**Preparation** (before the session):
- Distribute a brief survey: "What went well? What didn't? What would you change?"
- Compile survey responses anonymously
- Prepare a timeline of major events for reference

**Session Agenda**:
```
0:00 – 0:10  Welcome, ground rules, objectives
0:10 – 0:30  What went well? (celebrate — this is not a problem-hunting session)
0:30 – 0:60  What didn't go as planned? (facts only, no blame)
0:60 – 0:80  What should we do differently? (specific, actionable recommendations)
0:80 – 0:90  What to share with other teams? (identify highest-value insights)
```

### Categories to Cover
For each phase of the project, discuss:
- **Process**: Did our processes work? Were they followed?
- **Technical**: Technology choices, tools, architecture decisions
- **Team**: Communication, collaboration, skills, workload
- **Stakeholder**: Engagement, communication, requirements clarity
- **Risk**: What risks did we miss? What responses worked?
- **Scope**: How well did we control scope? What caused changes?
- **Schedule/Budget**: What caused variance? Were estimates realistic?

### Lessons Learned Output Format
For each lesson:
```
Category: [Process / Technical / Team / Stakeholder / Risk / Scope / Budget]
What happened: [Brief factual description — 1-2 sentences]
Why it happened: [Root cause — not symptoms]
Impact: [H/M/L — what was the effect on the project?]
Recommendation: [Specific action for next project — begin with a verb]
Priority to share: [H/M/L — how important is this for other teams to know?]
```

## Project Closure Report Structure

See `references/templates.md#closure` for the full template.

Key sections to make impactful:

### Executive Summary (1 paragraph)
Answer: Did the project succeed? What was delivered? What were the biggest challenges?
Be honest — stakeholders respect candor more than spin.

### Objectives Achievement Table
Map every SMART objective from the charter to its actual result.
Use ✅ (achieved), ⚠️ (partially achieved), or ❌ (not achieved) — with brief explanation.

### "What I Would Do Differently" Section
This is the most valuable section for the next PM. Write it as first-person advice:
"If I were starting this project again, I would..."

### Handover Notes
For projects delivering ongoing systems or services:
- Who now owns this? (Name, role, contact)
- What are the top 3 things the owner needs to know?
- Where is the documentation?
- What are the known limitations / technical debt items?

## Team Recognition & Celebration

Often skipped due to schedule pressure, and deeply regretted. Recognition is not a "nice to have" —
it's a retention and motivation investment for the next project.

### Recognition Ideas (scale to project size)
- Written thank-you from the sponsor (always do this)
- Team celebration (dinner, activity, awards)
- Individual performance highlights shared with managers
- LinkedIn recommendations or internal shoutouts
- "Project MVP" recognition
- Lessons learned contribution recognition

### What Makes Recognition Meaningful
- Specific (name the contribution, not just "great job")
- Timely (within 1-2 weeks of project close, not months later)
- Proportional (acknowledge the level of effort required)
- Witnessed (recognition in front of peers is more powerful than private praise)

### Documentation Archive Package
Include in the project archive:
- [ ] Project Charter (approved version)
- [ ] All approved plans (scope, schedule, budget, risk, communication, quality)

- [ ] Meeting minutes from key meetings
- [ ] Lessons learned register

## Post-Project Review (Optional — 3-6 Months After)

For high-impact projects, schedule a benefits realization review:
- Were the expected business benefits actually delivered?
- Are users/customers satisfied with the outcome?
- Is the system/product being used as intended?
- What additional value or improvements have been identified?

This closes the loop on the original Business Case and creates accountability for claimed benefits.
